Life Extension and Medically Assisted Dying: Two Sides of the Same Coin in Healthcare

23 August 2025

Introduction

This essay explores the ethical and political interplay between life extension and medically assisted dying, two practices that, despite appearing contradictory, share a foundational commitment to individual autonomy and self-ownership. Both challenge the natural order in pursuit of human agency. By situating them within the broader ideological spectrum—from left-wing advocacy for engineered societal outcomes to right-wing calls for limited intervention—this essay proposes a decentralised framework as a safeguard against coercive centralisation, ensuring that choices about life and death remain truly personal.

From Natural to Artificial: Political Philosophies of Order

The tension between natural and artificial order has long shaped human history. Natural order refers to the principle of letting life and systems evolve organically, without interference, emphasising individual freedom and self-determination. In contrast, artificial order entails human intervention to impose structure and control, often prioritising social constructs and collective goals. This philosophical divide has shaped political ideologies: right-wing movements typically favour natural order, advocating for limited government and personal liberty, whilst left-wing movements lean towards artificial order, promoting larger governments and systemic aims such as equality.

This divide is especially visible in healthcare policy. Right-wing ideologies tend to support minimal government involvement, leaving individuals to navigate their health decisions independently—often underpinning support for private healthcare. In contrast, left-wing ideologies advocate for comprehensive public healthcare systems that regulate and ensure access to services in the name of collective well-being. Life extension and medically assisted dying, as extensions of this debate, reflect the complex interplay between natural and artificial order.

Autonomy and Self-Ownership in Ethical Tension

At first glance, life extension and medically assisted dying appear to contradict each other. The former seeks to extend human life, whilst the latter facilitates its end. However, both practices advocate for the same underlying principle: autonomy. Life extension empowers individuals to choose longer lives, potentially breaking free from the constraints of biological ageing. Medically assisted dying ensures individuals can end their lives with dignity, free from prolonged suffering. Both practices challenge the natural order, advocating for a human-directed approach to life and death.

Crucial to this debate is the principle of self-ownership. Self-ownership posits that every individual has the inherent right to control their own body and, by extension, their lifespan. In the context of life extension and medically assisted dying, self-ownership means that every person has the right to decide how long they want to stay on earth, without having their decision judged or imposed upon by anyone else. Whether one chooses to extend life or end it, the decision should be recognised as a valid expression of agency. By asserting self-ownership, we affirm that such choices are an expression of individual agency—free from societal, governmental, or moralistic interference.

Similar to the advocacy for an equal right to live and grow older healthily, there is a parallel imperative to champion an equal right to die, regardless of one’s age or circumstances. “To live” and “to die” should be considered equally important rights, each rooted in the core belief that personal autonomy and self-ownership must be respected.

It is important to acknowledge that “to live” and “to die” possess an inherent practical asymmetry. Extending one’s lifespan through advanced life extension technologies is significantly more challenging than the relatively simpler process of ending life through medically assisted means. However, this disparity in difficulty should not be misconstrued as a difference in moral value between the two choices. Viewed through this lens, life extension and medically assisted dying are not mutually exclusive or oppositional practices; rather, they are ethically complementary.

Exploitation: Institutional Risks in End-of-Life and Longevity Care

Medically assisted dying has been a contentious topic for decades and remains illegal in most countries. Ethical debates focus on the sanctity of life, the risk of coercion, and the need for informed consent. Vulnerable individuals may face subtle or overt pressure to end their lives prematurely. In such cases, medically assisted dying risks becoming less an act of autonomy and more a response to structural despair or institutional neglect.

Life extension presents a different but equally complex set of ethical challenges. Whilst it is less likely to be directly exploited by individuals seeking to harm others, it remains vulnerable to exploitation by institutions, particularly centralised governments. In states facing demographic decline or economic stagnation, governments may view life extension not as a tool for personal flourishing but as a means to sustain national productivity. Policies may evolve to encourage or even subtly coerce individuals into prolonging their working years or delaying retirement, reframing longevity as a civic responsibility rather than a personal choice.

Although the exact nature of future life extension technologies remains uncertain, the possibility that authoritarian regimes might impose measures like “forced youth” or “forced longevity” cannot be dismissed. Conversely, medically assisted dying could be co-opted as a cost-saving measure within overburdened healthcare systems. This raises critical concerns about whether choices are truly voluntary—or shaped by fiscal logic rather than moral reasoning.

At root, these concerns return us to a core ethical question: Do we have value beyond our instrumental worth? If the answer is yes—as it must be—then both life extension and medically assisted dying must be governed by respect for human dignity. Neither should reduce people to tools for economic or political ends. The technologies of the future must serve individuals, not the reverse.

Toward a Decentralised Future: Political Technology for Personal Freedom

The desire to control one’s own life is universal—but it is also fragile, easily manipulated by systems of power. Nowhere is this vulnerability more acute than in decisions about life extension and medically assisted dying. To protect this autonomy, we must design a system that incorporates the aspirations of both ideological camps: the left’s optimism about expanding human capability through technology, and the right’s caution against government overreach.

This hybrid approach is essential to avoid the “road to serfdom”—a path where centralised authority quietly erodes freedom. A particularly dystopian risk is that life extension might be used by institutions to manipulate behaviour. Rejuvenation could become a conditional privilege, granted only to those who conform to ideological expectations, political loyalty, or behavioural norms. Worse still, life extension may be weaponised to deepen punishment—extending life not out of mercy, but to prolong sentences, extracting more labour and submission. In this scenario, the technology designed to delay death becomes a tool to delay release: a form of everlasting slavery.

The biological complexity of ageing demands long-term, large-scale research, which makes centralised institutions appealing. But institutions powerful enough to guide ageing research may also be tempted to extend their reach beyond science, shaping policy and civic behaviour under the guise of bioethical stewardship. What begins as planned research could morph into a system that imposes ideological conformity or limits personal freedom.

To mitigate these risks, we need a decentralised infrastructure—secure, transparent, and resistant to abuse. A practical path forward involves blockchain platforms that let individuals create verifiable records of their life-extension and end-of-life choices. Dynamic consent and smart contracts could ensure wishes are respected even if political contexts change. While such systems have risks, they are engineering challenges, not ethical flaws. What matters is that decentralisation offers a philosophically sound framework for protecting autonomy in an age of transformative biotechnologies.

Conclusion

The tension between natural and artificial order in healthcare, as embodied in life extension and medically assisted dying, compels us to rethink the limits of personal autonomy and self-ownership. Though these practices seem to move in opposite directions—one prolonging life, the other ending it—they both arise from the same fundamental impulse: the desire to govern one’s own existence. To recognise “to live” and “to die” as equally important rights is to affirm that ultimate authority over life must rest with the individual.

Yet autonomy cannot be safeguarded by technology alone. Life-extension technologies will almost certainly require institutions to fund, develop, and deliver them. That reality demands vigilance: institutions powerful enough to extend life are also powerful enough to shape how it is lived. A decentralised model, grounded in transparency, dynamic consent, and personal control, offers one way to prevent liberty from being subordinated to state interest or market pressure, ensuring that healthcare innovations serve the person, not the system.

But even with these safeguards, we must be clear about our long-term ambitions. Life-extension should remain a personal decision, not a collective mandate. The danger is that, without constant vigilance, we risk converting ourselves into cells that the Leviathans can choose to save or shed at their convenience.
